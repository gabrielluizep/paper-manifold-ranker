\begin{abstract}
Assessing the quality of telecommunications services across municipalities with heterogeneous structural, geographic, and socioeconomic conditions requires methodologies that go beyond simple weighted averages. This paper introduces a Graph Neural Network (GNN)-based manifold ranking framework that learns a latent representation of municipality-level telecom quality indicators and produces a unified ranking score. The proposed architecture employs Residual Graph Convolutional layers operating on a $k$-nearest-neighbor graph constructed from 18 infrastructure and quality-of-service indicators monitored by the Brazilian National Telecommunications Agency (ANATEL). The model is trained with a multi-objective loss function that simultaneously enforces pairwise ranking consistency, input reconstruction fidelity, geometric smoothness on the learned manifold, and directional alignment with domain knowledge. A monotonicity constraint is imposed on the scoring head to guarantee that improvements in underlying indicators always translate into higher scores. We validate the approach on a longitudinal dataset covering 4,088 Brazilian municipalities over 30 monthly periods (July 2022--December 2024), totaling over 82,000 municipality--period observations. Ablation studies across six experimental configurations demonstrate the contribution of each loss component. Temporal stability analyses via spaghetti plots reveal that the manifold ranker produces smoother and more consistent rankings than the regulatory baseline (IQS), while geographic visualizations confirm alignment with known infrastructure patterns. The framework provides a principled, data-driven alternative to expert-defined composite indices for regulatory quality assessment at national scale.
\end{abstract}

As the assumption of equality of service provisioning in multiple municipality stands improbable given the number of variable composed of, sample noise, differences of infrastructure, geographical distribution, etc., we can assume that when comparing two municipalities we always have one better than another. By repeating this process of comparison, and using the underlying transitivity property, we can define a ranking of municipalities service quality.

Since we don't have a definition or examples of which municipalities are better than other, and by how much, we try to bring the municipality similarity by using the \gls{iqs} in a \gls{knn} graph, where we have  the edges between the municipalities $\mathcal{E}$ representing the similarity between them.

\section{Dataset} \label{sec:dataset}

The dataset is organized as a panel indexed by two keys: \textit{month}, which
identifies the observation period, and \textit{municipality}, the official Brazilian
Institute of Geography and Statistics (IBGE) numeric code that uniquely identifies
each municipality. Each row therefore corresponds to one municipality--month pair.
The feature columns fall into four groups. The first group contains coverage
indicators: the 5G resident coverage variable reports the share of residents in the
municipality covered by a 5G signal, normalized by the total resident population,
while the 5G deployment indicator is a binary variable encoding whether 5G technology
is available in the municipality. The second group consists of composite quality
indices derived from the regulatory framework: $\mathrm{IBC}$ is the Index of
Coverage Quality, $\mathrm{ISG}$ is the General Satisfaction Index, and
$\mathrm{IQS}$ is the Quality of Service index, which aggregates the eight technical
indicators into a single score. The third group comprises the eight individual QoS
indicators defined under Anatel's RQUAL regulation~\cite{anatel2019rqual}, each
normalized to $[0,1]$ with higher values denoting superior performance:
$\mathrm{IND}_1$ (voice-call connection rate), $\mathrm{IND}_2$ (voice-call drop
rate, complemented), $\mathrm{IND}_3$ (data-connection rate), $\mathrm{IND}_4$
(download and upload speed compliance rate), $\mathrm{IND}_5$ (data-connection
latency, complemented), $\mathrm{IND}_6$ (jitter, complemented), $\mathrm{IND}_7$
(packet-loss rate, complemented), and $\mathrm{IND}_8$ (service availability rate).
The fourth group contains throughput measurements collected through Anatel's INF4
speed-testing infrastructure, reported separately by radio-access technology and
direction: $\mathrm{INF4}_{\mathrm{DL},3\mathrm{G}}$,
$\mathrm{INF4}_{\mathrm{DL},4\mathrm{G}}$, and
$\mathrm{INF4}_{\mathrm{DL},5\mathrm{G}}$ record median download speeds over 3G, 4G,
and 5G connections, respectively, while $\mathrm{INF4}_{\mathrm{UP},3\mathrm{G}}$,
$\mathrm{INF4}_{\mathrm{UP},4\mathrm{G}}$, and $\mathrm{INF4}_{\mathrm{UP},5\mathrm{G}}$
record the corresponding upload speeds.

\subsection{Legacy IQS}

The Anatel legacy composite, $\mathrm{QoS}_\mathrm{leg}$, aggregates the $K$ normalized
technical quality-of-service indicators through a fixed regulator-defined convex
combination,
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{QoS}_\mathrm{leg} = y_{\max} \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k^{\mathrm{leg}} \, \mathrm{IND}_k,
    \label{eq:qos_leg}
\end{equation}
where $y_{\max} = 10$ is the upper bound of the user satisfaction scale, each
$\mathrm{IND}_k \in [0,1]$ is a normalized technical indicator with higher values
denoting superior performance, and the weight vector $w^{\mathrm{leg}} \in \Delta^{K-1}$
lies on the probability simplex $\Delta^{K-1} = \{w \in \mathbb{R}^K : w_k \geq 0,\,
\sum_k w_k = 1\}$. Under the legacy specification, the weights are fixed at
$w^{\mathrm{leg}} = (0.1,\, 0.1,\, 0.1,\, 0.2,\, 0.1,\, 0.1,\, 0.1,\, 0.2)$,
assigning greater relevance to $\mathrm{IND}_4$ (download and upload speed compliance
rate) and $\mathrm{IND}_8$ (service availability rate), while distributing equal
weight among the remaining indicators.


\subsection{Revised IQS}

The Anatel revised composite, $\mathrm{QoS}_\mathrm{rev}$, introduced in 2025 through
the updated Reference Values Document (DVR)~\cite{dvr2025}, replaces the fixed weights
with a performance-dependent scheme combined with a coverage bonus. The indicators are
first ranked from worst-performing to best-performing within each municipality, and the
weight assigned to rank position $r$ is
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_r = \frac{1}{r \, H_K},
    \label{eq:alpha_r}
\end{equation}
where $H_K = \sum_{j=1}^{K} 1/j$ is the $K$-th harmonic number. The revised composite
is then defined as
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{QoS}_\mathrm{rev} = y_{\max} \sum_{r=1}^{K} \alpha_r \cdot \mathrm{IND}_{\pi(r)} + B_{\mathrm{cov}},
    \label{eq:qos_rev}
\end{equation}
where $\pi$ is the permutation ordering indicators from worst to best performance, and
$B_{\mathrm{cov}}$ is a coverage bonus corresponding to a fixed increment of five points,
added whenever coverage with 4G or higher technology in the municipality's urban area
reaches at least 95\%. When $B_{\mathrm{cov}} = 0$, the revised rule reduces to a linear
function of the indicators; when $B_{\mathrm{cov}} \neq 0$, it becomes affine. The
inverse-harmonic weighting assigns greater importance to the worst-performing indicators,
penalizing municipalities with more critical technical dimensions, in contrast to the
static weight distribution of the legacy composite.
